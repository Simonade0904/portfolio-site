HoneyCo Beekeeping

A group of designers who never raised honeybees before take on the challenge of designing a beekeeping dashboard.

Project duration: Sep. 2020 - Nov. 2020

Team: Me + 5 product designers

My role: Preliminary research, Information hierarchy, Prototyping

Timeline

The Beginning

In August 2020, HoneyCo approached our freelance design team for help. HoneyCo specializes in sensor hardware for bee hives. Beekeepers install their temperature and humidity sensors in hive frames to monitor the condition of the bee colonies.

We are tasked to build a software implementation of the hardware sensors, so that users can visualize all the time-series temperature data that the sensors can generate.

Problem Statement

How might we design the dashboard for beekeepers so that they can navigate through and access the information from the sensors with ease?

Becoming the Beekeeping Expert

Through Youtube videos, existing beehive-related apps, and user interviews, we learned all about the beekeepers' life.

Key Takeaways

  • Temperature is a key indicator of the bees’ health and honey productvity. One particular area of interest is the temperature of the hive’s center, where broods (bee larvae) are located.

  • After surveying a list of related mobile and desktop apps, we saw that graphs are essential for effective display of beehive temperature status. But another theme we identified is that provide more situational cues and context for the graphs to reduce a beekeeper’s mental load.

  • The total hive counts vary widely between individuals. So do the amount of time people can spend on each hive. Thus, our dashboard should be friendly to both a quick browse and more in-depth customizations.

Project Impact

At this point, we would have done a round of usability tests to validate our design, specifically on user comprehension of the spatial model and the graph interface’s learning curve. However, our client requested that we hand over the solution fast so they can continue to iterate upon the solution among the founders and the engineering team.

We maintained contact with the company for some time after shipping our design. Although we did not receive detailed feedback from the client, the majority of our design was well-received by both the limited user base of HoneyCo and the internal team. Upon further research and testing which I did not participate in, additional features such as a heatmap view and notepad were added to better suit a beekeeper’s real-life workflow.
